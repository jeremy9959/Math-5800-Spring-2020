Convolution¶

Convolution is a fundamental operation that appears in many different contexts in both pure and applied settings in mathematics. Let's look at a simple case first. Consider the space of real valued polynomial functions PP. If f∈Pf∈P, we have

f(z)=∑i=0∞aizif(z)=∑i=0∞aizi

where ai=0ai=0 for ii sufficiently large. There is an obvious linear map

a:P→c00(R)a:P→c00(R)

where c00(R)c00(R) is the space of sequences that are eventually zero. For each integer ii, we have a projection map ai:c00(R)→Rai:c00(R)→R so that ai(f)ai(f) is the coefficient of zizi for f∈Pf∈P. Since PP is a ring under addition and multiplication of functions, these operations must be reflected on the other side of the map aa. Clearly we can add sequences in c00(R)c00(R) componentwise, and since addition of polynomials is also componentwise we have:

a(f+g)=a(f)+a(g)a(f+g)=a(f)+a(g)

What about multiplication? We know that

an(fg)=∑r+s=nar(f)as(g)=∑r=0nar(f)an−r(g)=∑r=0∞ar(f)an−r(g)an(fg)=∑r+s=nar(f)as(g)=∑r=0nar(f)an−r(g)=∑r=0∞ar(f)an−r(g)

where we adopt the convention that ai(f)=0ai(f)=0 if i<0i<0.

Given (a0,a1,…)(a0,a1,…) and (b0,b1,…)(b0,b1,…) in c00(R)c00(R), define

a∗b=(c0,c1,…)a∗b=(c0,c1,…)
where
cn=∑j=0∞ajbn−jcn=∑j=0∞ajbn−j
with the same convention that ai=bi=0ai=bi=0 if i<0i<0.

This operation is called convolution; together with addition it makes the vector space c00(R)c00(R) into a commutative ring with identity element (1,0,0,…)(1,0,0,…). Furthermore by construction we have

a(fg)=a(f)∗a(g).a(fg)=a(f)∗a(g).

There are many generalizations of this. For example, suppose that we consider convergent infinite series instead of just polynomials. Since the product of convergent series is convergent and the coefficients are computed as if the series were (infinite) polynomials, we can extend convolution to this vector space.

If we consider Fourier Series as ℓ2ℓ2 summable sequences ∑∞n=0ane2πinz∑n=0∞ane2πinz then we have a linear map

F:L2(S1)→ℓ2(Z)F:L2(S1)→ℓ2(Z)
and F(fg)=F(f)∗F(g)F(fg)=F(f)∗F(g) so the coefficients of the Fourier series of the product of periodic functions is the convolution of the original functions' Fourier coefficients.

The convolution of two functions ff and gg on the real line is given by the integral

(f∗g)(θ)=∫f(ϕ)g(θ−ϕ)dϕ.(f∗g)(θ)=∫f(ϕ)g(θ−ϕ)dϕ.

This is a continuous version of the fourier series situation, and you can show that, with the right convergence conditions, that the fourier transform of the product of functions is the convolution of their fourier transforms.

An example¶

Suppose that f(x)f(x) is a function on the real line, and g(x)g(x) is a square bump

g(x)={100≤x≤1otherwiseg(x)={10≤x≤10otherwise
Then
(f∗g)(y)=∫f(x)g(y−x)dx=∫yx=y−1f(x)dx.(f∗g)(y)=∫f(x)g(y−x)dx=∫x=y−1yf(x)dx.
In other words the value of f∗gf∗g at yy is the average value of ff over the preceeding unit interval -- f∗gf∗g is a "moving average" of ff.

Convolution in two dimensions¶

Just like in one dimension, convolution is a type of filtering operation in two dimensions. The most direct generalization of the example at the beginning of this note would be to consider a function f(x,y)f(x,y) of two variables (our "image"), and take g(x,y)g(x,y) to be the function taking the value 11 on a square centered at the origin and zero elsewhere. Then the convolution f∗gf∗g would make a new image whose value at a point is the average of the values at the nearby points in a square region.

Let's grab a few MNIST images and do some experiments.

A gaussian filter means that instead of convolving our image with a "step function" we convolve with a Gaussian. This gives a weighted average for the pixel value at each point. The variance of the Gaussian affects the range over which the averaging is conducted. In the example below, we image our image sits in an infinite plane of zeros for purposes of computing the convolution.

Padding and stride¶

Padding: One question with filtering (or convolution) is how to handle boundary cases. Let's suppose for the moment that we are dealing with a discrete convolution kernel, like our 3x3 examples earlier, rather than a continuous one like a Gaussian. What happens when our 3x3 kernel hits an edge of our matrix?

  1. Only consider situations where the kernel fits entirely within the field of the image. If the image is NxM, and the kernel is HxW, then you can fit M-W+1 copies of the kernel in each row and N-H+1 copies of the kernel in each column. So the result of the convolution would have size (N-H+1)x(M-W+1).

  2. Pad the image (on each side, or top and bottom or both) by zeros -- in other words, allow the kernel to overlap the edge of the image, but assume the image is zeros outside of the original bounds. If we add p columns of zeros to each side of the image, then our padded image has M+2p columns and so we can fit M-W+2p+1 copies of the kernel horizontally; and similarly N-H+2p-1 vertically. Note: Commonly we can choose W=H, both odd, and then p=(W-1)/2, and we end up with our convolution producing output of the same size as the original image.

Stride: What if we move our filter by more than one step each time? The step size is called the stride s. In this case the output size is going to be (M-W+2p)/s+1.

A little math¶

Let's look more closely at the case of Fourier series. Let L2(S1)L2(S1) be the space of complex valued functions on the circle S1S1 (or the periodic complex valued functions on the real line) and suppose that ff is chosen from this space.

From Fourier theory, we know that f(θ)f(θ) has a Fourier expansion

f(θ)=∑n=−∞∞f^(n)einθf(θ)=∑n=−∞∞f^(n)einθ
where each coefficient f^(n)f^(n) is a complex number and ∑n∥f^(n)∥2<∞∑n‖f^(n)‖2<∞. The coefficients f^(n)f^(n) are given by
f^(n)=∫f(θ)e−inθdθ.f^(n)=∫f(θ)e−inθdθ.

Suppose that gn(θ)=einθgn(θ)=einθ. Then

(f∗gn)(θ)=∫f(ϕ)einθ−inϕdϕ=einθ∫f(ϕ)e−inϕdϕ=f^(n)einθ(f∗gn)(θ)=∫f(ϕ)einθ−inϕdϕ=einθ∫f(ϕ)e−inϕdϕ=f^(n)einθ

So convolution with a pure exponential projects a function onto one of its fourier components. Taking advantage of linearity and not worrying too much about convergence, we get the fundamental relationship between convolution and Fourier transform.

Proposition: The Fourier transform of a convolution is the product of the Fourier Transforms:

(f∗g)ˆ(n)=f^(n)g^(n)(f∗g)^(n)=f^(n)g^(n)

This principle holds true in many settings, accounting for the complications arising from convergence when the underlying space is not compact.

If we consider instead the Fourier Transform on RR, then the same principle holds -- Fourier transform changes convolution into product. At the same time, the Fourier transform of a Gaussian is again a Gaussian. So Convolution with a Gaussian, looked at in the Fourier domain, damps out the high frequencies of the image.

Fast Multiplication¶

Suppose you want to multiply two polynomials of degree d−1d−1. If f(x)=∑d−1i=0a(i)xif(x)=∑i=0d−1a(i)xi and g(x)=∑d−1i=0b(i)xig(x)=∑i=0d−1b(i)xi then the coefficients c(k)c(k) of the product are given by

c(k)=∑i+j=ka(i)b(j)=∑i=02da(i)b(k−i)c(k)=∑i+j=ka(i)b(j)=∑i=02da(i)b(k−i)
for k=0,…,2dk=0,…,2d. Notice that the sum on the right is the convolution of the two functions aa and bb; so thinking of these as functions on the positive integers we see that the kthkth coefficient of fgfg is (a∗b)(k)(a∗b)(k). Also notice this requires O(d2)O(d2) multiplications.

Let's think of aa, bb, and cc as functions Z/2dZ→CZ/2dZ→C.

We can:

  • compute the Fourier Transforms a^a^ and b^b^ of the functions aa and bb.
  • compute the pointwise product a^b^=c^a^b^=c^.
  • compute the inverse Fourier transform of c^c^ to recover the function cc.

This is only a good idea if it is more efficient then the direct multiplication. However, there is an algorithm known as the Fast Fourier Transform which computes a^a^ and b^b^, and then c^^c^^, in time O(dlogd)O(dlog⁡d), which is better than the O(d2)O(d2) of the original method. For dd large this gives a much more efficient way to multiply polynomials.

Applied to integers (which are sort of polynomials in powers of 10, or powers of 2) one gets a fast algorithm for multiplying large integers.
